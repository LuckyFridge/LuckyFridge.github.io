New Cert
by Rudy Portillo
27/Jun/21

Getting the A+ cert brings me two-thirds of the way to the CompTIA “trifecta”. It’s a bit of a cringe term, but at least industry folks know what I’m talking about when it stumbles out of my mouth during a Google Meet. I believe it’s CompTIA’s second-most “beginner” level cert, but my journey achieving it was absolutely harrowing… or at least half of it was.

I’d never intended to earn the A+ cert as I didn’t see its applicability to the cybersecurity field. Apparently, it’s VERY applicable, and with the new prevalence of side-channel attacks, it’s a good thing to have a solid foundation in computer hardware. It was a foundation I was sure I already possessed and was extremely cocky going into the official course and exam. The borderline arrogance was justified when I blew through the course and absolutely slam dunked the Core 1 portion of the exam. Hundreds of hours spent watching LTT computer building tutorials had finally paid off.

When Core 2 rolled around, I tried riding that wave of confidence as far as it would take me...which was not very far at all. The second part of the cert was all dense troubleshooting how-to guides, “computer safety” processes, and “professionalism” tips and tricks. I tried speedrunning it like I did the first section and all it produced was frustration and self-pity. I made the terrible decision to Hail Mary the exam and opted to take the at-home version. This was the most egregious mistake in a long line of epic blunders.

I was doing some light reviewing before my scheduled exam and about one hour before I had to login to PearsonVue, my home network had a Hindenburg-level meltdown. I scrambled to find any trickle of internet connection I could find. I eventually had to hardwire my laptop directly to my modem, bypassing my switch and router. When I finally logged on to the exam site, I had 3 mins to go through their maze of an onboarding process. To add to the pile, I tried to authenticate using a temporary state ID via a picture taken with my phone. When I finally got that mess untangled and was set to begin, the proctor couldn’t access my webcam and made me redo the entire onboarding process. I began the exam one and a half hours after my scheduled appointment.

I failed.

After settling my ID issues with a random county north of my city, and scheduling a second exam, I crammed for 5 days straight and took the test at an official testing center. I passed by the skin of my teeth on the second go around. Six measly points over the minimum score. I’d never been so happy.

I celebrated by streaming an equally poor performance on Warzone.

Now that the dust has settled, I’m certified and eligible to move on to my next semester. The next class is Network+ so hopefully I’ve learned my lesson on how NOT to study for CompTIA exams. I doubt it though.

As far as my site goes, I’ve made several minor updates:

  • Mild <hr> formatting
  • Updating the mailing address on my resume
  • Adding a fourth page to house my posts as they age
  • Upgrading the backend styling to Bootstrap 5
  • And of course updating the Certs page
  • I even updated the Copyright year in the footer of all the pages

I plan on making at least one more post before I complain about the Network+ exam. It’ll most likely deal with life post-Covid and how it’s affected my job and nearly everything else about the world. I may even talk about the various apocalypses Texas has managed to survive in the first 6 months of the year.

All things considered, life is looking good and I’m pretty optimistic in general. I’m even working on a non-professional site at luckyfridge.com. The plan is to have it be centered around my gaming and streaming hobbies.

So stay tuned. Cool things coming.

Promotion Post
by Rudy Portillo
1/Nov/2020

What’s it called when an employee…

Moves from middle management…

To upper management?

A promotion


Trash jokes aside, I did get a ‘promotion’, and it’s nowhere near management level. I’ve eeked my way out of the barren Service Desk landscape and wandered into the lush garden that is Quality Assurance. That’s right my friends, I now get paid to tell engineers and programmers “no”, and It feels great.

My superiority complex does take a hit when the defect turns out to be my testing methodology and not necessarily their bad coding, but for the time being, I can blame that on “being new”. All in all, it’s still sort of a dream-come-true to be paid to tell people, “sorry, this is unacceptable”.

While the department is nascent, there is a lot of ‘potential’ and other buzzword positivity stuff about our team. I enjoy working with everyone which, in any industry, is the absolute most you can ask for.

There was a pay increase, which was to be expected; but only served to remind me of the brutal income crisis our country is facing. After seeing a 34% pay increase, my high spirits were immediately cratered when I realized my income is essentially table scraps when viewed through a macro lens. I was disgusted with myself for being grateful for a miniscule slice of the Injustice Pie.

After fighting the urge to unionize, I concluded that I’m happy with my new position and consider it a great foundation for my future cybersecurity career.

New Cert
by Rudy Portillo
11/Oct/2020

I am finally ITIL V4 certified. I also have no idea what that means. Well, I know what it means, but I don’t know what it means. The study content itself reiterated the fact that the ITIL framework can be applied to every single organization, in every single industry, at every single corporate level, and “create value” if implemented properly. Those are some lofty promises and common sense tells me that one-size-fits-all synergistic management solutions are ‘suspect’ to say the least.

The ITIL framework consists of business concepts, that integrate with other business concepts, all wrapped up in a larger, more nebulous business concept. If that seems confusing, that’s because it is. So confusing in fact, that I failed my first attempt at the exam with a 40%. If it wasn’t for the tried-and-true Jason Dion I would have never passed.

That also points out that the coursework provided for the cert by WGU was completely useless. I was passing the ‘practice tests’ with 90s and even scored a 100 on my final practice before the official exam. I was so disheartened by the failure that I took a solid week off school to just play Warzone. With some feedback from other students on the WGU Cybersecurity Discord, I found myself dishing out $12 for Dion training that was guaranteed to get me a passing score. Turns out that 100% of the school’s coursework was only worth about 70% of the actual information I needed.

After going through the convoluted remote exam process, I was issued my gleaming new certification that never expires. I was pretty excited to tell my employer that I had a second cert to show off, but when I told them it was the ITIL V4, they shrugged. It did nothing to elevate my standing in the eyes of the government, so it didn’t even qualify for a mention in the Slack #announcements channel.

Education Update
by Rudy Portillo
29/Jul/2020

I officially began courses at Western Governors University on July 1, 2020. I am majoring in Cybersecurity and Information Assurance with an expected graduation date of December 2023.

When I first saw that expected date, my heart completely sank. I couldn’t understand how I’ve spent the last ten years in school in some form or another and only had one year worth of credits under my belt. I immediately ran through my transfer evaluation and did some quick napkin math… sure enough… I only transferred enough credits to be considered a sophomore.

I shot off a hasty, yet wordy email to my ‘program mentor’ inquiring as to why I was expected to attend, for a full three years, a school that purported to have ‘accelerated’ programs. Turns out I don’t read the fine print… or listen during orientation… or pay attention in general… because the posted graduation date is a worst-case-scenario deadline for completion. The date is calculated with the supposition that the student is progressing at a snail’s pace, which I am definitely not.

The courses at WGU are structured such that, as soon as you finish one course, you can move immediately to the next one, given you’ve passed the course assessment. Each course has its own study schedule that’s recommended for a timely completion, but moving faster is not only common, but encouraged.

As it stands today, I’m about one month ahead of schedule. If everything goes to plan and my life doesn’t experience any seismic shifts, I should have the degree completed in about eighteen months. It’s all very exciting, as I consider this my THIRD attempt at a bachelor’s degree.

As more time passes, I’m starting to realize that I may be part of the extremely small percentage of people whose lives were actually enhanced by the pandemic. Without the lockdown, I wouldn’t be working from home, and I wouldn’t be able to create the time or environment here at home specifically for study.

I’m tempted to invoke Fate here, but for now I think I’ll chalk it up to being a “glass half full” kinda guy.


CoV-2
by Rudy Portillo
24/Jul/2020

It’s been three months since my last update, and to be honest, morale hasn’t improved. I ended my last post with a pseudo-hopeful platitude about being grateful despite all that was going on in the world; and while that hasn’t changed, I can’t ignore the bleakness of our future. In a mere 90 days, things went from bad to worse. A thick fog of misinformation has settled over everything and thinking critically about our situation is turning into a full-time job in and of itself. The most upsetting part of all this is that the most predictable thing has happened: People have turned on each other. Whether this is out of fear or ignorance, I don’t know. There now may not be a distinction between the two.

I guess the most obvious example to point out would be the sheer amount of conspiracy theories cropping up on social media. Some claims are simply rehashed versions of the old “blame the government” trope and are generally harmless. But others are so absurd and comical that you can’t help but think the ones pushing these ideas are in on the joke. My favorite theory in this category is the “5G causes COVID-19” claim. The mental gymnastics required to link cell towers to a novel virus are impressive to say the least. I like seeing weirdos post maps of 5G towers overlaid with coronavirus hotspot maps to present the similarity as rock-solid proof of their ideas. It’s even more fun to see their bubbles burst when other people point out that those maps also correlate to population density, which of course explains why the other two maps look almost the same. But people don’t like to be wrong, and the conversation inevitably devolves into a depressing tirade of insults.

Less amusing though, is the disagreement over face coverings. I still cannot believe that this concept became politicized. I can’t understand the mind of someone who would hear the logical advice of “cover your face so your spit doesn’t get everywhere” and think, No, you can’t make me do that. From what I can tell, there are as many reasons why people won’t mask up as there are anti-maskers themselves. The most annoying people are those who cite their Constitutional freedoms as a free pass for not having to use a mask. 1) The virus does not discriminate based on nationality. 2) The virus does not affect people differently based on their level of patriotism. 3) Your Constitutional rights essentially end where mine begin. WEARING A MASK KEEPS OTHERS SAFE! After doing some research, the consensus since May of this year is that: at worst, simple homemade masks only block a certain percentage of aerosols of a certain size or bigger. At best, they block spit droplets at a high enough rate that there are numerous examples of the virus not spreading even in non-socially distanced situations. I’ve included a quote from May regarding the effectiveness of face coverings.



Mandating face mask use in public is associated with a decline in the daily COVID-19 growth rate by 0.9, 1.1, 1.4, 1.7, and 2.0 percentage-points in 1–5, 6–10, 11–15, 16–20, and 21+ days after signing, respectively. Estimates suggest as many as 230,000–450,000 COVID-19 cases possibly averted By May 22, 2020 by these mandates.

—Wei Lyu and George L. Wehby



It’s important to note that hindering the virus’ spread is a three-pronged approach involving masks, hand washing, and social distancing. But for whatever reason, face coverings became the flashpoint, which is why I highlighted their effectiveness.

The more sinister side of this mask denial is that some people are intellectually dishonest about the situation, and will downplay the severity of the virus, in general, to support their views. Personally, I find that morally reprehensible. They’ll claim mortality percentages prove that the whole thing is “overblown” and that everyone is going to “catch it eventually”. Because of their self-centeredness, they refuse to understand that even a small percentage of 300,000,000 people is a large number. And it’s a number that represents actual humans dying, and there’s an even larger number of them actively suffering.

At the very least, these anti-mask crusaders acknowledge the existence of the virus as a fact. Some people think even that notion is going too far and won’t accept that there’s anything to be concerned about in the first place. For me, this is where the conspiracy theories regain some of their lightheartedness, simply because I view these people as ‘unreachable’ by common sense or logic. A good time-waster is to search YouTube with the term “COVID Karen”. You’ll be served with results dealing with anti-maskers like the ones mentioned earlier, a few Sovereign Citizens, and of course, the people equating COVID-19 in general to Nazi Germany or some New World Order. When they start blaming George Soros or the Rothschilds, you’ll know you’ve hit some prime tinfoil hat conspiracy content.

This isn’t to say detractors don’t have valid points. They often cite skyrocketing rates of depression and domestic violence as reason enough to end the lockdown. I’d have to agree. To me, quarantine always felt like a best option out of a pile of terrible options. Unfortunately, my only counter to these arguments is that these problems have always existed in our community, and it shouldn’t have taken a global pandemic for us to acknowledge them.

There are a million other things coronavirus-related that need to be discussed, but they’re far beyond the scope of this particular post. I could go on all day about how remote schooling for children is the best idea, but that idea has to be squared with the fact that poor kids will be adversely affected, just as they are in every other terrible situation. I could also talk about how the initial lockdown made the virus worse for low income minorities because they are more likely to live in larger groups and in substandard conditions, which itself points to more egregious and systemic social ills.

Ultimately, I still have hope. But these days, that sentiment is hollower than I want it to be. I thought everyone’s shared humanity would be reason enough to come together and address an existential issue with reason, but, as usual, my faith in humanity was misplaced.
